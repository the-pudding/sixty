The study received coverage across dozens of outlets. The headline: A person‚Äôs ability to be random peaks around 25 years old and declines after 60.

Thankfully the researchers made their data and methods public, allowing us to explore them on our own. We initially set out to use the findings to make an age guessing game, but after our analysis (and the fact that it might only perform slightly better than a coin flip) we realized that it wasn‚Äôt our best idea.

For what its worth, we have guessed right 0% of the time. Not as good as we had expected üòî.

This story is now about the replication crisis (the ongoing concern that it is hard to reproduce many scientific studies). According to the article, ‚Äúone 2015 attempt to reproduce 100 psychology studies was able to replicate only 39 of them.‚Äù

We wanted to reproduce the study for a couple of reasons. As indicated above, replication is important, plus seeing how random you are is fun! Also, we think the findings are at the mercy of a single decision the researchers made to include questionable responses. To us, this meant the participant either misunderstood the instructions, or intentionally sabotaged the experiment.

This decision was the difference between ‚Äúah, this is interesting‚Äù and ‚Äúwelp, nothing here‚Äù (or ‚Äúnull results‚Äù in statistics lingo). To be clear, this decision isn‚Äôt about negligence, but rather the unavoidable personal opinion that informs the methodology. Our opinions diverge at this point, so we wanted to break down its consequences more deeply.

Let‚Äôs dive in. Here is the wording of the instructions from in the original study:

Click on a number between one and six as randomly as possible to produce the kind of sequence you‚Äôd get if you really rolled a die, so that if another person is shown your sequence of digits from 1 to 6, he/she should not be able to tell whether these numbers were produced by a real die or just ‚Äúmade up‚Äù by somebody.

The study was peer-reviewed, but to our knowledge it hasn‚Äôt been reproduced. This is typical, since reproduction takes a significant amount of time and effort on the part of other scientists busy conducting their own research.

We aren‚Äôt peers, but here is our review: There are two camps around the decision to filter out questionable responses. We believe that there are obvious responses that are candidates for removal that make the data more true. The researchers believe that you can only analyze the raw responses because, statistically, any sequence is equally likely to occur, so where do you draw the line? To them, filtering any data would be tampering with the results.

What do you think about these two takes on the decision? Regardless of your opinion (or ours or theirs), we wanted to make it explicit that no study is black-and-white, and it is always worth exploring those gray areas.

The calculation of algorithmic complexity is done using the CTM method. In practice, we use a custom implementation of the acss R package to obtain this complexity for given user inputs and compute an overall score.

For the task level data, we use the original dataset of the study and use a cutoff of non-uniform responses to remove questionable ones. We then overlay this data with a trend line calculated using the LOESS (locally estimated scatterplot smoothing) method.

The original study asked users to perform five tasks, but since the results are basically the same across the board, we decided to reduce our experiment to three tasks because of attention spans (not yours, it is exceptional if you are reading this).
